## Press Release
## Inter-agence calls for support for people fleeing Yemen

Nairobi, 9 December 2015 - The International Organization for Migration (OIM) and the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) launched an inter-agence call for a total of $94,130,731 to provide protection and assistance to refugees, repatriates and migrants fleeing the conflict in Yemen in 2016.
Mr. Mohamed Abdulkarim A. Assad, Business Officer of the Embassy of Yemen in Kenya, was the honorary guest of the event that gathered several participants including ambassadors, donors and representatives of various organizations.
Johannes Van Der Klaauw, UN humanitarian coordinator in Yemen, explained to the participants the humanitarian consequences of the conflict that worsened in March 2015.
It is estimated that 80% of the population needs protection and assistance, including the 2.3 million internally displaced people. In addition, about 170,000 Yemenians, refugees and third-country nationals fled Yemen to Djibouti, Ethiopia, Somalia, Sudan and some Gulf countries.
I hear direct testimonies every time I speak to women, children and elderly people who have undertaken the dangerous journey to Djibouti, Ethiopia and Somalia,” said Claire Bourgeois, the regional coordinator of the UNHCR for refugees in the context of Yemen.
She greeted neighboring countries for asylum and assistance to refugees, repatriates and migrants, and donors for their support, and concluded, “But our joint efforts must continue and intensify so that refugees can recover their rights and lead a decent life in the asylum country.
The IMO’s permanent observer at the United Nations in New York, Mr. Ashraf El Nour, emphasized the need for the IMO and the UNHCR’s commitment to the side of governments for a comprehensive response and inclusive dialogue in order to capture the migration and the humanitarian dimension of the crisis. “The corner of Africa remains among the hot points of mixed migration movements composed of refugees, migrants in trouble and people living in food insecurity. However, in 2015, the financing of the response to new arrivals is less than 50% of the estimated needs. This could promote a shift beyond the first host country,” he said.
